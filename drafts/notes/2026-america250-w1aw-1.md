From July 21 through July 28, 2026, I operated as W1AW/1 from Rhode Island
during one of the state's two weeks in ARRL's America250 Worked All States
event. I signed up because Rhode Island had only a small group of interested
operators. I wanted Rhode Island to show up well: not necessarily on the air
every minute, but present often enough, across enough modes and times, that
people looking for W1AW/1 had a reasonable chance to find us.

Most of my planned time was CW, with two SSB blocks mixed in. I added FT8 after
getting the existing WSJT-X and QK4 setup working with the event call. The
initial plan was to keep sessions to one or two hours, use 40 meters for the
first part of the morning, move to 20 meters as it opened, and reverse that
progression in the evening. Short, repeatable sessions felt more useful than
one heroic run followed by several quiet days.

## Planning a week instead of one activation

My first concern was not finding one good operating period. It was spreading
coverage across the week without conflicting with another Rhode Island station
using the same mode. The operator guidelines allow at most three W1AW/Portable
stations from a state at the same time: one CW, one Phone, and one Digital.
That makes the conflict rule statewide by mode, not merely a duplicate
band-and-mode check.

With only a few operators expressing interest, every additional block helped.
I did not expect to fill the entire week myself, and trying would have been a
good way to burn out early. Instead, I aimed for a mix of mornings and evenings,
all three mode categories, and band changes that followed propagation. The
measure of success was useful coverage and clean contacts, not pretending that
Rhode Island had a twenty-four-hour operation.

## Making the coordination visible

Email was enough to recruit operators, but it was a fragile way to maintain a
week-long schedule. I made a shared Google Sheet with a short README and a
Schedule tab containing one row per 30-minute period. Separate CW, Phone, and
Digital columns made the statewide mode limit visible without requiring a
separate approval step: entering a call sign and band claimed the slot.

The sheet was not an attempt to become the organizer. It was a small way to
reduce the coordination burden and make it easier for each interested operator
to contribute whatever time they had. Even one claimed half-hour made the
state's coverage a little less dependent on one person guessing where the gaps
were.

I initially sent the sheet only to the coordinator. By Wednesday morning, with
the event already underway, I sent it to the full invited group so operators
could coordinate directly. I added the invitation addresses as editors;
Google could not match two of them to Google accounts, so the group email also
asked anyone who received a view-only sheet to send the Google-account address
they wanted added.

## The logging requirement mattered

This was not an event where I could scribble contacts on paper and reconcile
them later. The guidelines require electronic logging in ADIF format during
the operation, periodic backups, and complete station-location data in every
QSO record, including county, grid square, state, CQ zone, and ITU zone.

Making Rhode Island look well represented was not just a matter of putting a
signal on the air. It also meant avoiding duplicate contacts where practical,
recording the actual operating details, and delivering a log the coordinator
could use without an archaeological dig afterward. A smaller number of clean,
confirmable contacts would serve the event better than a larger but unreliable
log.

After each operating period, the written procedure says that the operator
uploads the ADIF file through the first-stage utility. That utility validates
the log and adds the operating location -- state, county, grid, CQ zone, and
ITU zone -- to every QSO. The state coordinator then reviews and combines the
operators' logs. ARRL HQ performs the final LoTW upload because it holds the
W1AW call-sign certificates.

## What I want to keep

- Short sessions spread across different days, times, bands, and modes were
  sustainable and gave more people a chance to find Rhode Island.
- A simple self-service schedule lowered the cost of contributing. Operators
  could add a half-hour or two without starting another email round trip.
- Switching bands or modes when activity made the original plan unproductive
  was better than treating the schedule as a promise to remain on one frequency.

## What I want to improve

- Start coordination earlier, with the shared schedule already available before
  the operating window opens.
- Verify every logging profile, station field, transmit-power value, and radio
  memory before the first session. Scarce operating time is more useful on the
  air than spent reconstructing configuration details.

### Facts verified from the operator guidelines

- Each state receives two seven-day periods running Wednesday at 0000 UTC
  through the following Tuesday at 2359 UTC.
- All amateur bands are permitted except 60 meters; WARC bands are explicitly
  allowed.
- The event recognizes the United States Semiquincentennial through the
  America250 Worked All States award and related 2026 ARRL activities.
- At most three W1AW/Portable stations may be on the air from a state at once:
  one CW, one Phone, and one Digital.
- Operators are encouraged to call `CQ WAS-250` on CW or Phone. A duplicated
  later paragraph in the document instead says `CQ 250WAS`; confirm the
  preferred on-air wording with the coordinator if it matters to the article.
- Operators should ask twice whether a frequency is in use and move without
  arguing when a conflict appears.
- Frequencies to avoid include 14.300 MHz, 14.325 MHz, 7.268 MHz, and 7.262
  MHz, along with band and license-class edges.
- W1AW/Portable contacts are confirmed through LoTW; ARRL HQ does not provide
  paper QSL cards for the portable event stations.
- Electronic ADIF logging during the event is mandatory, paper logs are not
  accepted, and periodic backups are required.
- Each QSO must carry complete station-location data such as county, grid,
  state, CQ zone, and ITU zone.
- Operators perform the first log-upload step after their activations. The
  state coordinator combines the operator logs and submits the final log
  within ten days. ARRL HQ signs and uploads the W1AW logs to LoTW.

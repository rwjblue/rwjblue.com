![Shaded picnic-table operating position at Lyndon Baines Johnson Memorial Grove on the Potomac](/images/pota/2026-06-21-washington-dc-pota-rove/lbj-picnic-table-station.jpg)

The Sunday DC part of the [Rhode Island-to-Florida POTA
rove](/projects/2026-06-rove-to-fl/) was the high-count day: a walking route
around the memorials, then a final drive over to [Lyndon Baines Johnson Memorial
Grove on the Potomac, US-0784](/radio/pota/US-0784/) for the big seven-reference
finish.

The Sunday-local route added 106 QSOs after the late-night C&O block. Combined
with the Saturday-night UTC activation at [Chesapeake and Ohio Canal National
Historical Park, US-0015](/radio/pota/US-0015/) and [George Washington Memorial
Parkway, US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/), which I covered in the [day two rove
note](/notes/2026-06-20-rhode-island-to-florida-rove-day-two/), the full UTC day
had 16 unique references in my ADI. The POTA Rover Cheetah threshold is 15
references in one UTC day, so that was enough for the Cheetah certificate.

For context, the rover animal awards step up by reference count: Rhino at 10,
Cheetah at 15, and Ostrich at 20 references in one UTC day. I was briefly
surprised that a Cheetah run did not also produce a Rhino, but they are separate
award paths.

## At a glance

- **Where:** DC memorial route plus the LBJ seven-reference site near Columbia Island
- **When:** June 21, 2026; roughly 1300-2300 UTC for the Sunday-local stops
- **Activation:** 106 QSOs on the Sunday-local route; 75 CW, 29 SSB, and 2 FM on 40, 20, 2 meters, and 70 centimeters
- **Cheetah context:** 16 unique UTC-day references in the ADI; 15 needed for POTA Rover Cheetah
- **Radios:** Elecraft KX2 for the walking CW stops; FT-891 for the higher-power SSB/CW work later in the day
- **Antennas:** 25-foot whip with a 40-meter coil, Rybakov-style whip setups, KJ6ER Challenger, AX1/AXE, and the SpoolTenna Ultra 40/20/10 EFHW at LBJ
- **CW gear:** VK3IL paddle for the early KX2 stops, KXPD2 paddle at Lincoln, and Zippy paddle with the FT-891 at LBJ

## [Thomas Jefferson National Memorial, US-0792](/radio/pota/US-0792/) and [National Mall, US-0655](/radio/pota/US-0655/)

The day started later than planned because Max and I had gotten into DC late the
night before. The original Sunday plan had LBJ first, but with the late start I
did not want to get the car, drive over there, activate, and then solve DC
parking all over again before walking the memorial route. I let Max sleep in at
the hotel and got myself over to the Jefferson Memorial area around 8:45 a.m.
local instead.

For the first stop I used the KX2 at 5 watts, the VK3IL paddle, and the
25-foot whip with the 40-meter coil. I did not need the full whip length to get
the match down around 1.2:1, so the setup was compact enough for the shaded
grass without feeling like I was building a station in the middle of a memorial
site.

I made 12 CW QSOs on 40 meters in just under half an hour, starting a little
after 1300 UTC. That was exactly the kind of pace I needed: get the first two
references activated, prove the walking kit worked, and keep moving.

## [Franklin Delano Roosevelt Memorial, US-0776](/radio/pota/US-0776/) and [National Mall, US-0655](/radio/pota/US-0655/)

At FDR I met up with Paul, [KD3ALB](https://www.qrz.com/db/KD3ALB). Paul is a
good friend from work about 15 years ago, and he has lived in DC for roughly the
last decade. He came out to join the rove for the day, and this stop was the
first chance to talk while I operated.

I stayed with the KX2 at 5 watts and used the 25-foot whip in a Rybakov-style
vertical setup with four 33-foot radials. It took more space than the
Challenger, but the shaded operating spot had enough room for it. I made 10 CW
QSOs on 40 meters in about half an hour.

## [Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial, US-0785](/radio/pota/US-0785/) and [National Mall, US-0655](/radio/pota/US-0655/)

The MLK stop was another compact walking activation. I used the KX2 with the
KJ6ER Challenger, made 10 CW QSOs on 20 meters in roughly half an hour, and had
no real station problems.

This was also where Max caught up with us after sleeping in. By the time he
arrived, I was nearly done and starting to pack. Paul and Max hit it off
quickly, which helped the rest of the day. There is a lot of waiting around on a
rove like this, especially when the operator is trying to finish a slow band
with a small antenna.

## [Korean War Veterans Memorial, US-0780](/radio/pota/US-0780/) and [National Mall, US-0655](/radio/pota/US-0655/)

At the Korean War Veterans Memorial we found shade and a bench, which made the
stop feel much less exposed than it could have. I used the KX2 again, but
switched to the KJ6ER Challenger on the tripod. The Challenger is an
off-center-fed dipole built around the 25-foot whip as the long radiating
element, with a short linked tail for band changes. It has a much smaller
horizontal footprint than the Rybakov or loaded quarter-wave setups, which
matter when there is not much room for 33-foot radials.

I made 11 CW QSOs on 20 meters from about 1600 to 1645 UTC. Conditions were not
spectacular, but the station was workable and the shaded operating position made
the stop comfortable.

There was also a small-world moment. A couple of passersby stopped to ask what
we were doing, and one of them had lived in Ocala, Florida, years ago. That
connected oddly well with Paul, Max, and me: Paul and I worked together there,
Paul went to high school there, and Max was born there.

## [Lincoln Memorial, US-0783](/radio/pota/US-0783/) and [National Mall, US-0655](/radio/pota/US-0655/)

Lincoln was the hard stop. The memorial is surrounded by broad open space, sun,
and people. It did not feel right to operate directly from the memorial itself,
and the surrounding grass did not have the shade or low-key operating position I
wanted.

We spent far too long trying to ask a ranger what was reasonable. She was kind
and trying to help, but I think the question sounded more like a formal event
than three people with a small radio. That led to an attempted call to park
communications and burned roughly an hour before I finally disengaged and
shifted to the simplest possible station.

The actual activation used the KX2 with the AX1/AXE. That was convenient, but
very compromised. I switched between 40 and 20 meters and had to swap the coil
configuration as I went. The 40-meter signal was weak enough that I apparently
was not showing up on RBN, even though hunters were still finding me.

I eventually got 10 CW QSOs: six on 40 meters and four on 20 meters. It felt
longer than it was. The KX2 internal battery died during the process, the phone
battery was also getting low, and I ended up using a small Powerpole-to-USB-C
buck converter to keep the phone alive. The clip-on KXPD2 paddle was the right
paddle for that awkward standing operating position.

There was a real propagation wrinkle too. [SIDC later
reported](https://www.sidc.be/products/xut/) an M6.8 flare from NOAA AR 4473
peaking at 19:29 UTC, and [NOAA SWPC described the
event](https://www.spaceweather.gov/news/r2-event-31-june-ar-4473) as an R2
radio blackout. That peak was after my last Lincoln contact, so I would not
blame the entire stop on the flare, but the band was already rough and the
timing lines up with the part of the afternoon that felt like a slog.

## [Vietnam Veterans Memorial, US-0793](/radio/pota/US-0793/) and [Constitution Gardens, US-0653](/radio/pota/US-0653/)

The Vietnam Veterans Memorial stop saved time. I had planned Vietnam and
Constitution Gardens as separate activations, but the right spot near the
walking path let us activate both references together.

Paul and I did this one as a joint activation, which made it his first POTA
activation. We set up on the ground in the shade with the 25-foot whip in a
Rybakov-style configuration and four 33-foot radials. After the Lincoln
struggle, SSB moved faster and the better antenna got us back on track.

We made 20 SSB QSOs in about an hour: seven on 20 meters and 13 on 40 meters. A
few contacts were surprising. We logged Spain and the Dominican Republic while
some nearby stations could not hear us at all. Rory,
[W8KNX](https://www.qrz.com/db/W8KNX), also found me on sideband and joked
about having to find his microphone after so much CW. I also finally got
[KC1VIO](https://www.qrz.com/db/KC1VIO) in the log. He is in my radio club, and
we have talked a bunch about him trying to hunt me, but Massachusetts and Rhode
Island are usually too close for easy HF paths.

The best part was watching Paul get through the first-activation nerves:
calling, answering questions from passersby, and seeing that the public-facing
part of POTA is usually just a short friendly explanation.

## The LBJ seven-reference finish

I would have loved to get LBJ done the night before, but our timing into the DC
area was just too late. By late afternoon Sunday I had to choose between
continuing the walking route and protecting the LBJ stop. The original bigger
plan still had more possible memorial references, but the LBJ site was too
valuable to risk. It counted for seven references from one operating position:
[Lyndon Baines Johnson Memorial
Grove on the Potomac, US-0784](/radio/pota/US-0784/), [Mount Vernon,
US-9898](/radio/pota/US-9898/), [Potomac Heritage Trail,
US-4564](/radio/pota/US-4564/), [George Washington Memorial Parkway,
US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/), [Captain John Smith Chesapeake,
US-4567](/radio/pota/US-4567/), [Civil War Defenses of Washington,
US-9901](/radio/pota/US-9901/), and [Star-Spangled Banner National Historic
Trail, US-4581](/radio/pota/US-4581/).

That decision also meant giving up on an Ostrich attempt for the day. A Rhino
would already have been a personal best, and the Cheetah was still possible if
LBJ worked. I did not want to burn the remaining daylight on another walking
stop and then fail to finish the seven-reference activation before the UTC
rollover.

Paul came with us. We took an Uber back to the hotel, got the car, loaded the
larger station again, and drove over to LBJ. The site was excellent: open grass,
big shade trees, and picnic tables. [W3LAM](https://www.qrz.com/db/W3LAM) had
given me very useful local guidance before the trip, and the operating area was
exactly the kind of place he described.

We set up at a picnic table with two antennas available: the KJ6ER Challenger
and the SpoolTenna Ultra 40/20/10 EFHW in a tree. I started on SSB with the
Challenger and the bands finally sounded alive again, but the vertical was also
picking up a weird rhythmic clicking from some man-made QRM source. Once we
switched to the EFHW, we stayed there instead of going back and forth.

The other ergonomic problem was noise from airplanes passing overhead. Reagan
National is right there, and when a plane went over, we simply had to tell
callers to wait because we could not hear.

At LBJ we made 33 QSOs in about an hour: 19 SSB, 12 CW, one 2-meter FM contact,
and one 70-centimeter FM contact. The FT-891 CW contacts used my Zippy paddle.
One of the first SSB contacts was [SM3NRY](https://www.qrz.com/db/SM3NRY) in
Sweden, which was a great way to start the final stop. The gray-line timing
probably helped, but after the hot, slow middle of the day, it felt good to have
the band come back.

We stayed at LBJ for about an hour and a half, which was longer than most of
the stops but worth it. The site was comfortable, Paul got another activation,
and the seven-reference finish turned the day from a good DC rove into the
Cheetah run.

## What worked

- Starting with small KX2 CW stops kept the walking route light enough to move
  through the memorials without the car.
- The small tripod and 3/8-24 adapter worked well for public-space verticals. I
  did not stake anything into the ground; when the whip needed wind support, I
  threaded it through tree branches or used the gear bag to weigh down the
  windward tripod leg.
- The antenna footprint mattered. The Rybakov and loaded quarter-wave setups
  needed room for four long radials, while the Challenger kept the horizontal
  footprint small when the operating spot was busy.
- Finding the Vietnam / Constitution Gardens overlap recovered time after the
  slow Lincoln stop.
- Switching to SSB for the joint activation with Paul made it much easier to
  help a new activator get into POTA.
- The LBJ site was every bit as useful as advertised. Local advice from
  [W3LAM](https://www.qrz.com/db/W3LAM) made the seven-reference finish feel
  planned instead of improvised.

## To adjust next time

- Ask site-permission questions in simpler physical terms first: where can I
  sit, what can touch the ground, and how small is the setup. Leading with radio
  details can make a tiny station sound like an event.
- Bring an audio splitter for shared SSB operating. Headphones would have helped
  at LBJ, but Paul and I both needed to hear.
- Treat the AX1 as a last-resort activation tool on a high-count rove. It can
  work, but it turns a marginal band into a long stop.
- For a Cheetah-plus attempt, protect the highest-value n-fer earlier. The LBJ
  finish worked, but it was too important to leave exposed to late-day delays.

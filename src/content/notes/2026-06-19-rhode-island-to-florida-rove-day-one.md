The [Rhode Island-to-Florida POTA
rove](/projects/2026-06-rove-to-fl/) started with three parks on the first day:
[Rockville Wildlife Management Area, US-6991](/radio/pota/US-6991/), in Rhode
Island, [Silver Sands State Park, US-1716](/radio/pota/US-1716/), in
Connecticut, and [Tallman Mountain State Park, US-2149](/radio/pota/US-2149/),
in New York.

I finished the day with 56 QSOs in the log: 11 at Rockville, 15 at Silver Sands,
and 30 at Tallman Mountain. The pattern was useful for the rest of the trip:
the fast car-based station worked, the coastal wind exposed a weak point in the
hitch-supported Challenger setup, and taking the FT-891 out to a picnic table at
the evening stop made the activation feel a lot less like a rushed travel chore.

## At a glance

- **Where:** [Rockville Wildlife Management Area, US-6991](/radio/pota/US-6991/), [Silver Sands State Park, US-1716](/radio/pota/US-1716/), and [Tallman Mountain State Park, US-2149](/radio/pota/US-2149/)
- **When:** June 19, 2026 local time; the Tallman Mountain log crossed 00:00 UTC on June 20
- **Activation:** 56 QSOs total; 39 CW and 17 SSB on 40, 30, and 20 meters
- **Radio:** Yaesu FT-891
- **Power:** 60 watts on CW and 75 watts on SSB; 50 Ah LiFePO4 battery in the car, with a 12 Ah Bioenno battery that needed troubleshooting at Tallman Mountain
- **CW gear:** N3ZN QRP-Lite II paddle
- **Antennas:** PackTenna 29-foot end-fed random wire with a 17-foot counterpoise at Rockville; KJ6ER Challenger at Silver Sands and Tallman Mountain

## [Rockville Wildlife Management Area, US-6991](/radio/pota/US-6991/)

Rockville was the first stop and the first real test of the rove workflow. I
used the FT-891, the PackTenna 29-foot end-fed random wire, a 17-foot
counterpoise on the ground, and a feedpoint choke. The setup was quick: about
six minutes from arrival to being ready to call CQ, and that included realizing
that I had forgotten to hook up the choke and fixing it before getting on the
air.

The whole stop took about 50 minutes, including setup, operating, and teardown.
That is about right for the kind of travel-day activation I was hoping for. The
log shows 11 CW QSOs from 13:31 to 13:59 UTC: five on 40 meters, one on 30
meters, and five on 20 meters.

## [Silver Sands State Park, US-1716](/radio/pota/US-1716/)

Silver Sands was the difficult stop. I used the FT-891 again with the same
N3ZN paddle, but switched to the KJ6ER Challenger. That antenna is an
off-center-fed dipole built around a 25-foot whip for the long side, a linked
short side for band changes, and a 4:1 transformer.

The problem was noise. The receiver was sitting around S7, and it was not clear
whether the source was the car, nearby power lines, or something else in the
area. Prior testing and the Rockville stop both made me doubt that the Tesla
running the air conditioning was the main cause, but the result in the chair was
the same either way: I could tell people were calling on CW, but I often could
not pull them out.

I also tried to use the hitch-supported tripod setup from the shakedown at Big
River. At Silver Sands, the coastal wind was enough to show the weak point. I
had a Voile strap from the tripod mast to the hitch-mounted flagpole holder, but
the single hinge point let the whip rotate around the strap instead of staying
braced. I think that setup needs at least two straps, probably one higher and
one lower, before I trust it in real wind. For this activation I went back to
the simple stake-in-the-ground setup, which worked.

I started on CW while fighting the FT-891 noise settings and eventually enabled
IPO so the receiver was not being driven quite so hard. That helped, but it also
meant I was not hearing as well as I was getting out. After a few CW contacts and
a lull, I switched to SSB hoping that more hunters would be strong enough to get
through the noise. That worked. The final log was 15 QSOs on 20 meters from
16:56 to 17:33 UTC: six CW and nine SSB.

## [Tallman Mountain State Park, US-2149](/radio/pota/US-2149/)

Tallman Mountain was the best stop of the day. The views across the Hudson
toward the Tappan Zee Bridge were excellent, the picnic area was comfortable,
and the evening weather was just about perfect: roughly 80 degrees, breezy, and
pleasant in the shade.

KM3STU met us at the park after a few emails back and forth about where to set
up and how the park worked. He activates Tallman Mountain often, and it was good
to have local knowledge instead of guessing. He stayed for maybe 45 minutes
while we set up, and the conversation was a big part of why this stop was fun:
LICW, my CW progress, Max and college, car-based portable operating, his
portable setup, and the usual exchange of field-radio ideas that only really
happens when activators meet in person. Before he left we also got a P2P in the
log, which made the stop feel more like meeting another activator than just
checking New York off the route.

For this one I took the FT-891 out of the car and set it up on the picnic table.
The antenna was the KJ6ER Challenger, configured only for 20 meters. It was
resonant and ready to go, and once the radio was powered I had contacts almost
immediately.

The odd failure was power. I had grabbed the 12 Ah Bioenno battery that had
been on the charger, and the charger had gone from red to green like I
expected. In the field, though, the radio would not turn on from that battery at
all. We tried a different power cable before I gave up, went back to the car,
and brought over the larger 50 Ah battery.

The operating made up for the battery puzzle. The log has 30 QSOs on 20 meters
from 23:12 UTC on June 19 to 00:29 UTC on June 20. Because the activation
crossed the UTC day boundary, the contacts split into 18 on June 19 UTC and 12
on June 20 UTC. I do not remember the exact split while operating, but it was
enough to make the stop count cleanly on both UTC days.

Most of the Tallman Mountain contacts were CW, but I switched to SSB for a bit
just to give it a whirl. WD5EEI has a very distinctive CW signature, and I
usually know it is him before I finish copying the call. Hearing him call on
sideband was great. We were both slumming it on voice, but it was fun to
actually chat. Another station called through a remote rig only a couple of
miles from the park, which was also a neat local twist.

The final Tallman Mountain tally was 22 CW QSOs and eight SSB QSOs, all on
20 meters.

## Power notes

The in-car power setup was one of the more interesting technical pieces from the
day. I had a small Powerpole distribution block with the car's 12 V outlet
feeding an 8 A DC-DC LiFePO4 charger, the 50 Ah battery, and the radio all tied
together. I also had meters on the output from the car and the output from the
battery, so I could see whether the battery was actually carrying the activation
or mostly buffering the radio during transmit.

After Rockville, the 50 Ah battery had only given up about 0.5 Ah, while the
DC-DC charger had supplied 5.5 Ah from the car. That was exactly the behavior I
was hoping for: the battery covered the higher draw when I transmitted, but the
car still carried most of the energy for the stop. I forgot to check the meter
after Silver Sands, but after Tallman Mountain the battery had given up another
6.5 Ah.

The 12 Ah Bioenno battery remains the weird part. After it would not power the
radio at the start of the Tallman Mountain activation, I put it on the charger
in the car while I operated from the 50 Ah battery. When I was packing up, I
tested the Bioenno again and it powered the radio normally. It was sitting at
14.45 V, which is basically full for a LiFePO4 pack. So that one was not simply
a dead battery; I need to figure out whether it was a connector, BMS wake-up, or
some other field-power oddity.

## What worked

- The Rockville workflow was fast enough for a travel-day rove: six minutes to
  set up, 50 minutes total for the stop, and 11 contacts in the log.
- The Powerpole distribution block and DC-DC charger setup let the 50 Ah battery
  buffer transmit current without carrying the whole activation by itself.
- The PackTenna end-fed random wire remained the dependable quick option when I
  wanted to minimize setup complexity.
- Taking the FT-891 out to the picnic table at Tallman Mountain made the evening
  activation more comfortable than operating entirely from the car.
- The N3ZN QRP-Lite II paddle worked well across all three stops.

## To adjust next time

- The hitch-supported Challenger setup needs a better wind brace before I use it
  near the coast again. One strap was not enough.
- If a site has S7 noise and CW callers are just below copyable, moving to SSB
  sooner may be the better activation strategy.
- The 12 Ah Bioenno battery needs a real check before it goes back into the
  travel kit. A green charger light was not enough information.

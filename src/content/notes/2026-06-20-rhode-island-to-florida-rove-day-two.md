![Hitch-mounted mast and wire set up from the shaded Cheesequake State Park parking lot](/images/pota/2026-06-20-rhode-island-to-florida-rove-day-two/img-9308.jpg)

The second local day of the [Rhode Island-to-Florida POTA
rove](/projects/2026-06-rove-to-fl/) started from Edison, New Jersey, and kept
moving south: [Cheesequake State Park, US-1611](/radio/pota/US-1611/), [John
Heinz National Wildlife Refuge, US-0512](/radio/pota/US-0512/), [Fox Point State
Park, US-1738](/radio/pota/US-1738/), and a late twofer for [George Washington
Memorial Parkway, US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/) and [Chesapeake and Ohio Canal
National Historical Park, US-0015](/radio/pota/US-0015/).

I finished with 51 radio QSOs across those five references. Cheesequake set the
tone for the day: a shady parking spot, the hitch mount, the DX Commander
Expedition pole, and the 29-foot random wire were all ready to go in about five
minutes. After that the day got less tidy. John Heinz was beautiful but slower
than planned, Fox Point turned into a long and worthwhile activator meetup, and
the original Greenbelt timing fell apart. The finish was a legal-access pivot to
the Anglers Lot on the C&O Canal after the UTC rollover.

## At a glance

- **Where:** [Cheesequake State Park, US-1611](/radio/pota/US-1611/), [John Heinz National Wildlife Refuge, US-0512](/radio/pota/US-0512/), [Fox Point State Park, US-1738](/radio/pota/US-1738/), [George Washington Memorial Parkway, US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/), and [Chesapeake and Ohio Canal National Historical Park, US-0015](/radio/pota/US-0015/)
- **When:** June 20, 2026 local time; the final twofer crossed into June 21 UTC
- **Activation:** 51 radio QSOs total; 50 CW and 1 SSB on 40, 30, and 20 meters
- **Radio:** FT-891 at Cheesequake, Fox Point, and the late twofer; KX2 at John Heinz
- **Antenna:** DX Commander Expedition pole as a 10-meter mast with a 29-foot random wire and 17-foot counterpoise for the quick wire setup
- **CW gear:** Paddle on a longer cable for the in-car operating position; leg strap used later in the day

## [Cheesequake State Park, US-1611](/radio/pota/US-1611/)

Cheesequake was the first activation of the day and a good way to start. We
found a shady parking spot near the Multi-use White Trail, put the hitch mount
on the car, raised the DX Commander Expedition pole, and ran the 29-foot random
wire with a 17-foot counterpoise. The whole setup was ready in about five
minutes, which is exactly the kind of pace I wanted for a travel-day rove.

I made 16 QSOs from 13:49 to 14:37 UTC: six on 40 meters and ten on 20 meters.
Fifteen were CW, and I made one SSB contact near the end. The first contact was
[W8KNX](https://www.qrz.com/db/W8KNX), Rory from Everyday Ham, which was a
great way to start the day. A few contacts came very quickly after that, and
then I hunted around a bit, moved to 20 meters, and called CQ there.

[VO1AW](https://www.qrz.com/db/VO1AW), Brian in Newfoundland, also found me on
20 meters. I can usually tell it is him as soon as I hear the leading "V" in the
call, and it is always good to work him.

The in-car operating position was better than day one. I had a longer cable for
the paddle, so I could sit straighter instead of twisting around to send. At
Cheesequake I was still balancing the paddle on my leg, which worked but felt a
little precarious. Later in the day I got the leg strap out for Fox Point and
C&O, and that was the better setup.

After the activation, Max and I took the station down and walked the trail for a
bit. It was a gorgeous day, and the trailhead parking spot made the whole stop
feel less like a parking-lot errand and more like a park visit.

## [John Heinz National Wildlife Refuge, US-0512](/radio/pota/US-0512/)

John Heinz was the Pennsylvania stop, and Max and I decided to hike in instead
of doing another car-adjacent setup. The weather was gorgeous, and the refuge
being right there in the middle of the city was really cool. It felt strange in
the best way: a real urban wildlife refuge, but still a proper place to get off
the road for a bit.

I used the KX2 at 5 watts on 20 meters and made 11 CW QSOs from 17:24 to
18:52 UTC. That was longer than I wanted for the schedule. We had enough time
overall, but I was hoping this would be a quicker activation, and it put us a
little behind for the rest of the day.

This was a different feel from the Cheesequake car setup: lower, quieter, and
more like a short sit-down field activation than a quick in-car run. I had the
KX2 down near the wire and mast with the operating position tucked into the
shade.

The first contact was exactly the kind of contact I like on a rove: I hunted my
buddy Justin, [N8JMS](https://www.qrz.com/db/N8JMS), for a park-to-park. After
that it was quiet for a while, but getting him first made the slow start a lot
better. I also picked up several more park-to-park contacts at John Heinz,
including [AI4ZD](https://www.qrz.com/db/AI4ZD),
[AC1NM](https://www.qrz.com/db/AC1NM), [K0BUF](https://www.qrz.com/db/K0BUF),
and [VE2KLW](https://www.qrz.com/db/VE2KLW). That helped the stop feel connected
to the broader Saturday POTA traffic instead of just being a state checkbox on
the drive south.

## [Fox Point State Park, US-1738](/radio/pota/US-1738/)

Fox Point was the Delaware stop, maybe 20 or 30 minutes from John Heinz. I used
the FT-891 in the car with the end-fed random wire on the DX Commander
Expedition mast in the hitch mount. The mast collapsed right as I was getting
ready to start calling CQ because I had not extended the sections firmly enough.
That was annoying, but simple enough to fix.

I had been emailing with Jay, [N3RT](https://www.qrz.com/db/N3RT), before the
trip because he activates Fox Point often and lives nearby. He was extremely
helpful with local advice, and he came out to meet us at the park. Before he
arrived, I made one 40-meter contact with Perry,
[W1GRD](https://www.qrz.com/db/W1GRD). Perry is the one who met me in
Massachusetts and let me try his KX2 and IC-705 before I decided which radio to
buy, so that was a good one to get from Delaware.

When Jay arrived, we ended up standing outside and talking for a long time. He
brought his dog Pumpkin, who was excellent. We talked about Max going to
college, his kids, James, radio, getting licensed, getting back into the hobby,
and a lot of portable gear. That is one of my favorite parts of meeting other
activators: seeing what they actually use and hearing why it works for them.

Jay uses a 25-foot whip on a triple magnet mount on his car. I do not think that
would work well on my car because the body panels are not the right kind of
metal for a mag mount and the roof is glass, but it was interesting to see how
well it works for him. With the tuner in his rig, he can make that setup cover
what he needs from the car.

Jay was going to activate too, so we picked bands that would not fight each
other. He was planning to use 20 meters, and even though he let me choose first,
30 meters made more sense for me. He also moved his car about 150 feet away from
mine because he expected that operating right next to me could desense my
receiver even on different bands, which is not something I would have thought
about.

I made 12 CW QSOs from 20:10 to 22:25 UTC: one on 40 meters before Jay arrived,
then eleven on 30 meters after we finished talking. The 30-meter run was the
useful part of the stop. [N3RT](https://www.qrz.com/db/N3RT) hunted me from
across the way, and the rest of the activation came together in about 40 minutes
once I got going.

The leg strap was also sorted out by this point, and that made the CW operating
position steadier than the balancing act at Cheesequake. The harder problem was
noise. I had horrendous noise on 20 meters at Fox Point, while Jay did not. That
points back at something about my car setup, not the park. At C&O later, with
basically the same radio and antenna setup, the noise was not there in the same
way. It would spike briefly and then drop away.

I need to debug that more carefully the next time it happens. It might be cable
routing, where the coax passes through or near something noisy in the Tesla, or
some other car state I have not isolated yet. I already had the counterpoise and
choke on the end-fed random wire, which helped in earlier testing at home, so
the next round of debugging probably needs to include moving the radio outside
the car, changing cable routing, and trying to correlate the noise with specific
car systems.

Fox Point put Delaware into the day-two record, but it also cost time. Between
the longer John Heinz stop and the long conversation at Fox Point, we were well
behind the original plan.

## [George Washington Memorial Parkway, US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/) and [Chesapeake and Ohio Canal National Historical Park, US-0015](/radio/pota/US-0015/)

The original plan was to get to Greenbelt around the 00:00 UTC turnover, but
that was not going to happen. We still had roughly two hours of driving, the car
was down around 25 percent, and Max was hungry. We stopped to charge, walked
about three quarters of a mile to a steakhouse, got Max a steak, and did not
leave Delaware until about 8 p.m. local time.

That put us into Maryland around 10 p.m. local, and the Greenbelt access we had
planned to use closes at dusk. There is 24-hour access with the right parking or
camping permit, but we did not have one. I try to be careful about POTA access:
activate from a place I am actually allowed to be, and do not bend the site
rules just because the route plan says a stop would be convenient.

So I pivoted to the Anglers Lot at [Chesapeake and Ohio Canal National
Historical Park, US-0015](/radio/pota/US-0015/). A lot of the C&O Canal access
points are not 24-hour lots, but Anglers is, presumably because people fish at
odd hours. It also counted as a twofer with [George Washington Memorial Parkway,
US-0670](/radio/pota/US-0670/).

I set up the end-fed random wire again and used the FT-891 with the N3ZN paddle
and leg strap. I started at 02:46 UTC on June 21 and made 12 CW contacts: eight
on 40 meters and four on 20 meters.

The contacts came fast, which was exactly what I needed at that point. I was
very tired. Rory, [W8KNX](https://www.qrz.com/db/W8KNX), found me again, and
then he also worked me as [W8EDH](https://www.qrz.com/db/W8EDH), the Everyday
Ham club call. When I texted with him later, he said he could definitely tell I
was tired, so he figured he could give me another contact and help me get out of
there sooner. That was both funny and accurate.

[KM3STU](https://www.qrz.com/db/KM3STU) also found me again, which was excellent
after meeting him at Tallman Mountain on day one. The surprise DX was
[M0TTQ](https://www.qrz.com/db/M0TTQ) in England. He was just starting his own
activation as a park-to-park, which made for a neat late-night contact from the
C&O Canal.

## What worked

- The Cheesequake wire setup was fast enough for the rove pace: hitch mount,
  mast, random wire, and counterpoise in about five minutes.
- The longer paddle cable made the in-car operating position much more
  workable.
- The paddle leg strap made the later CW stops steadier once I finally got it
  out.
- Switching bands kept the day moving: 40 and 20 meters at Cheesequake,
  20 meters at John Heinz, 30 meters at Fox Point, and 40/20 meters for the late
  twofer.
- Meeting Jay at Fox Point was worth the schedule hit. The local advice, gear
  comparison, and desense warning were all useful.
- The late twofer did exactly what it was supposed to do for the larger route:
  add the Maryland/DC-area references after the UTC rollover while staying
  inside legal access hours.

## To adjust next time

- Put the paddle leg strap somewhere obvious in the car kit so I use it from the
  first stop instead of remembering it halfway through the day.
- Debug the Tesla noise when it appears again: move the radio outside the car,
  change the coax route, and try to correlate the noise with specific car
  systems.
- Be more realistic about hike-in activations during travel days. John Heinz was
  worth doing, but it was not a quick stop.

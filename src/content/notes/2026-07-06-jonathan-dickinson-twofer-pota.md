![Robert and Randy KO4WTM at the Kitching Creek Pavilion sign in Jonathan Dickinson State Park](/images/pota/2026-07-06-jonathan-dickinson-twofer-pota/robert-randy-kitching-creek-pavilion.jpg)

I met [Randy, KO4WTM](https://www.qrz.com/db/KO4WTM), at [Jonathan Dickinson
State Park, US-1887](/radio/pota/US-1887/) on Monday morning. The operating
spot was also inside [Loxahatchee River National Wild and Scenic River,
US-6281](/radio/pota/US-6281/), so the stop counted as a twofer from the same
picnic-table setup.

This was one of the best parts of the Florida trip. Before heading south I had
emailed Randy to ask about activating [John D. MacArthur Beach State Park,
US-1884](/radio/pota/US-1884/) and a few other parks in the area. He was
generous with local advice, connected me with the West Palm Beach Amateur Radio
Group for Field Day, and also pointed me toward the [Jupiter Tequesta Repeater
Group](https://jtrg.org/) Field Day site at Carlin Park. Getting to finally
meet up and operate together was the whole point of the morning.

I finished my log with 11 CW QSOs on 20 meters at 5 W. The band was not easy:
lots of QSB, uneven signal strength, and a slow start before 20 meters opened a
little more. NOAA's planetary K data showed the actual July 6 operating window
was quiet, but the previous couple of days had been disturbed enough that the
recent space weather was still on my mind. The radio was only part of the
morning, though. We spent a few hours talking antennas, local radio, parks, and
portable operating, which is still my favorite part of POTA.

## At a glance

- **Where:** [Jonathan Dickinson State Park, US-1887](/radio/pota/US-1887/) and [Loxahatchee River National Wild and Scenic River, US-6281](/radio/pota/US-6281/), Florida; Kitching Creek Pavilion area
- **When:** July 6, 2026; we met around 8:30-8:45 a.m. local, and my log ran from 14:37-15:09 UTC
- **Activation:** 11 CW QSOs on 20 meters, all at 5 W
- **Radios:** Randy's Yaesu FT-818 for the first part; my portable station for the final 11-QSO run
- **Antennas:** Randy's EFHW on two crappie poles, then my KJ6ER Challenger setup for 20 meters
- **Power:** 5 W
- **CW gear:** Randy's Ashi paddle, I think from N6ARA, and a Bama Tech TP-III; my N3ZN portable paddle with the KX2 for my station

## Field notes

Randy and I met near the Kitching Creek Pavilion and claimed a picnic table. We
intended to operate, but the first couple of hours were mostly conversation. He
has activated there many times, and it was useful to hear how he thinks about
the nearby parks, local clubs, and simple field setups.

His antenna setup stuck with me. He had a pair of roughly 20-foot crappie poles
and a small Spark Plug-style EFHW transformer. The wire went up one pole for
maybe 15 feet, across horizontally, and then down the other pole for another
15 feet or so. The result was not exactly a half square, but it had that same
useful shape: two vertical sections with a horizontal section between them,
taking up much less left-to-right space than a full-length wire.

I want to model that antenna later. Without doing the actual modeling, my guess
is that the vertical portions are doing something useful for the takeoff angle,
and the whole arrangement is much easier to fit into a busy park than a long
wire stretched out in one direction. Randy also had sand-style fishing-rod
holders screwed into the ground as pole supports, which made the setup tidy and
fast.

We hunted a bit and then called CQ with Randy's FT-818. It is a neat little QRP
radio: HF, 2 meters, and 70 centimeters in a small all-band package. He had a
tuner with it, though the EFHW did not seem to need much help. He also had the
whole radio setup in a good-sized Apache case, and the case worked as an
elevated stand for the FT-818. That sounds small, but it kept the display and
controls high enough that we were not craning down at the picnic table the whole
time. The band was up and down enough that we only put a few contacts in his log
before changing antennas.

There was also a quick non-radio interruption while we were setting up. A park
ranger checking around the pavilion found a juvenile raccoon that had climbed
into one of the deep trash cans and could not get back out. The can was too deep
to reach into safely, so we found a long fallen limb, set it down into the can,
and backed off. Once things quieted down, it climbed out on its own.

After the slow start we switched to my KJ6ER Challenger setup for 20 meters.
The antenna seemed to do better, but I am not sure how much of that was the
antenna and how much was timing. It was later in the morning by then, and 20
meters may simply have opened up a bit more. The numbers back that up more than
my memory of a stormy band: NOAA's daily geomagnetic data has the July 6
planetary K values around 1.33-1.67 while I was on the air, after a G3-level
July 4 and an active July 5. Either way, I called CQ for around 30 or 40 minutes
and got 11 contacts in my log, including one Canadian station and ten U.S.
stations. One of those was my buddy Tim, [K5OHY](https://www.qrz.com/db/K5OHY),
from Texas. He is a great operator, and hearing him send a [proper
roger](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/vQuyDHZ7Wv0) on a bug is always a treat.

Once my log had enough contacts, I handed the frequency back to Randy for a bit.
I think he added a couple more but did not get a fresh activation completed,
which he was not worried about because he has activated the park plenty of times
before. We kept talking through teardown, and by the end we had probably spent
three hours together at the park.

That is the part I keep coming back to. The contacts were useful, the twofer
counted, and the antenna ideas are worth stealing, but the real win was meeting
another portable operator who had already helped make the whole Florida radio
trip better.

## What worked

- Meeting Randy in person turned a normal twofer into a useful local-radio and
  antenna conversation.
- The two-crappie-pole EFHW setup was compact, quick to support, and worth
  modeling before I copy it.
- Randy's Apache case made a useful raised operating stand for the FT-818.
- The Ashi, Bama Tech TP-III, and N3ZN paddles were all useful references for
  portable CW ergonomics. The TP-III especially felt excellent, which is
  inconvenient because they are basically impossible to get now.
- The KJ6ER Challenger gave me enough 20-meter performance to finish the
  activation once the band improved.
- A picnic-table operating position kept the morning relaxed and made it easy
  to swap between stations.

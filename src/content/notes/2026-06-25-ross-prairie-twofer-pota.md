The Thursday Florida stop on the [Rhode Island-to-Florida POTA
rove](/projects/2026-06-rove-to-fl/) turned into a morning twofer for [Ross
Prairie State Forest, US-4638](/radio/pota/US-4638/) and [Marjorie Harris Carr
Cross State Park, US-3637](/radio/pota/US-3637/). It was close to my mom's
house, maybe 10 or 15 minutes away, and it gave me a chance to get outside
before work instead of treating the Florida part of the trip as only a travel
and family stop.

I finished with 13 CW QSOs from a single operating position: seven on 40 meters,
four on 30 meters, and two on 20 meters. The bands were uneven, but the twofer
counted for both references and gave me a quiet hike-in activation in a place
that felt very familiar and very different at the same time.

## At a glance

- **Where:** [Ross Prairie State Forest, US-4638](/radio/pota/US-4638/) and [Marjorie Harris Carr Cross State Park, US-3637](/radio/pota/US-3637/), Florida
- **When:** June 25, 2026; about 12:49-14:06 UTC, or 8:49-10:06 a.m. local
- **Activation:** 13 CW QSOs on 40, 30, and 20 meters; three park-to-park contacts
- **Radio:** Elecraft KX2
- **Antenna:** 51-foot end-fed random wire, thrown roughly 35-40 feet into a tree and sloped back toward the operating position
- **Power:** Mostly 5 W from the KX2 internal battery, then 12 W from an external Bioenno battery for the last two contacts
- **CW gear:** Zippy paddle and headphones

## Field notes

I left around 8 a.m. with coffee and headed for the Ross Prairie trailhead. The
parking area was close, but I wanted the activation to be a hike-in instead of
another car-adjacent stop. I walked about three quarters of a mile in before
setting up. It was warm and humid, but not awful yet. The sun was already coming
up hard enough that the morning had a clear timer on it.

For the station I brought the KX2, the Zippy paddle, and the 51-foot end-fed
random wire. I do not have a dedicated counterpoise for that wire right now, so
I used the coax as the counterpoise: about 20 feet of coax running back to the
operating position, with the tiny CMC choke at the radio side.

The throw was one of the better ones I have had in a while. The wire went up
around 35 or 40 feet, over a limb, and then back down at roughly a 45 to 60
degree angle. That left the wire as a broad inverted-V shape, with the coax
sloping back down toward me.

I started on 40 meters, moved to 30 meters, and then moved to 20 meters. The
log ran from 12:48:49 to 14:05:39 UTC. The contact pace was slow and uneven:
sometimes a station would be 5 by 9 plus 10, then barely register on the meter a
few minutes later. There was a lot of empty CQ time, but enough hunters found me
to get the activation done. Three of the contacts were park-to-park.

Most of the activation was at 5 W on the KX2 internal battery. Somewhere around
9:30 or 9:40 local the internal battery quit, which was not too surprising. I
do not think I fully recharged it after the last beach activation. I plugged in
the external Bioenno battery and kept going. The KX2 will apparently do 12 W
from an external battery, even though it will not do 12 W from the internal
pack, so the last two contacts in the log were at 12 W.

The useful battery note is still rough. Based on this and the prior activations,
I think the internal KX2 battery is somewhere around four and a half to five
hours of practical POTA CQ operating for how I am using it, but I need a more
deliberate test before trusting that number.

This was also a Pack Mule attempt, but not a finished one by itself. I had the
hike distance, but only 13 QSOs. I may be able to finish the award with a
consecutive-day follow-up, but even if I do not, the hike-in part was worth it.

The non-radio part of the morning was the best part. I sat in the prairie and
woods with coffee, saw some big spiders, and as I was cleaning up saw a hawk
perched right above the operating position. I had
sprayed picaridin before leaving, which was the right call.

There was also a personal layer to being there. The landscape felt like the
Florida I remember, but I was experiencing it in a way I mostly did not back
then. I was not as healthy, and being outside in the heat was uncomfortable
enough that I rarely got out like this. Sitting there with the KX2 and a wire in
a tree was a small but very concrete reminder that things are different now.

## What worked

- The hike-in made the stop feel like a real park visit, not just a local
  errand before work.
- The 51-foot wire throw was excellent, with the high point around 35-40 feet
  and a useful inverted-V shape back toward the operating position.
- The coax-as-counterpoise setup, with the choke at the radio side, was enough
  to get the twofer done.
- Carrying the external Bioenno battery saved the activation after the KX2
  internal battery quit.
- Picaridin before leaving the house kept the bugs manageable.

## To adjust next time

- Fully recharge the KX2 internal battery after every activation instead of
  relying on memory.
- Run a deliberate KX2 internal-battery test for POTA-style CQ operating so the
  runtime estimate is based on a real measurement.
- Bring or build a dedicated counterpoise for the 51-foot end-fed random wire.
- If I want Pack Mule from this kind of morning stop, plan enough time for the
  higher QSO count or pair it deliberately with a consecutive-day follow-up.

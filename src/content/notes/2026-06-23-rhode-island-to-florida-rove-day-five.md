![KX2 and 25-foot whip set up on the beach at Jekyll Island State Park](/images/pota/2026-06-23-rhode-island-to-florida-rove-day-five/img-9406.jpg)

Day five of the [Rhode Island-to-Florida POTA
rove](/projects/2026-06-rove-to-fl/) started in South Carolina and ended on the
beach at Jekyll Island. The plan was to get South Carolina in the log before
relying on the state-boundary ambiguity at [Savannah National Wildlife Refuge,
US-0522](/radio/pota/US-0522/), then finish the day from [Jekyll Island State
Park, US-0636](/radio/pota/US-0636/), where the hotel was technically inside
the park.

I finished with 46 CW QSOs across three references: [Woods Bay State Park,
US-2915](/radio/pota/US-2915/), [Savannah National Wildlife Refuge,
US-0522](/radio/pota/US-0522/), and [Jekyll Island State Park,
US-0636](/radio/pota/US-0636/). Woods Bay was a quick 40-meter car activation,
Savannah was a shaded picnic-table stop with the KX2, and Jekyll was a
20-meter gray-line run from the beach with salt water in front of the antenna.

## At a glance

- **Where:** [Woods Bay State Park, US-2915](/radio/pota/US-2915/) in South Carolina, [Savannah National Wildlife Refuge, US-0522](/radio/pota/US-0522/) at Abercorn Landing, and [Jekyll Island State Park, US-0636](/radio/pota/US-0636/) in Georgia
- **When:** June 23, 2026; roughly 1324-1343 UTC at Woods Bay, 1655-1738 UTC at Savannah NWR, and 2315-2348 UTC at Jekyll Island
- **Activation:** 46 CW QSOs total; 14 on 40 meters and 32 on 20 meters
- **Radios:** FT-891 at Woods Bay; KX2 at Savannah NWR and Jekyll Island
- **Antennas:** DX Commander Expedition mast with the PackTenna 29-foot random wire and 17-foot counterpoise for Woods Bay and Savannah; 25-foot whip in a Rybakov-style setup at Jekyll Island
- **Power:** 60 W at Woods Bay; 5 W at Savannah NWR and Jekyll Island
- **CW gear:** Paddle setup not recorded in my notes

## [Woods Bay State Park, US-2915](/radio/pota/US-2915/)

We got out of the hotel around 8:15 a.m., grabbed breakfast and coffee, and
pointed the car toward Woods Bay. The extra South Carolina stop was deliberate:
Savannah NWR spans Georgia and South Carolina, and I did not want the larger
all-states goal to depend on exactly where we ended up operating there.

Woods Bay was a neat rural South Carolina park with a pavilion and visitor area.
The pavilion looked tempting, but there were enough biting flies around that I
kept the activation car-based instead. I used the DX Commander Expedition mast
in the hitch-mounted flagpole holder, the PackTenna 29-foot random wire, a
17-foot counterpoise, and a choke at the feed point.

For an early activation, 40 meters was the right call. During the morning, 20
meters can easily land where a lot of hunters are still asleep, while daytime
40 meters gives a better close-in footprint. I only had to call CQ once or
twice before hunters started finding me.

I made 14 CW QSOs on 40 meters from 13:24 to 13:43 UTC. The whole stop was
short, maybe 30 or 40 minutes including setup and teardown. Max was on the phone
with Jacquie while I was operating, and when a small pileup formed he called
out, "Oh, that's more than one person at a time." He is probably going to pick
up CW faster than I did if he ever decides to try.

## [Savannah National Wildlife Refuge, US-0522](/radio/pota/US-0522/)

After Woods Bay we headed for Savannah NWR. Bill,
[W4ZDP](https://www.qrz.com/db/W4ZDP), and Stacy,
[WA4IDS](https://www.qrz.com/db/WA4IDS), had both pointed me toward Abercorn
Landing, and that was great advice. It was a quiet boat ramp inside the refuge
boundary with a picnic table, shade, and almost nobody else around.

Max backed the car up near the picnic table, and I used the same mast and random
wire setup while operating from the table with the KX2. I kept the power at 5 W.
The band was a little squirrely, and I had a noisy spot around 14.049 MHz,
possibly from the car or something nearby. I moved a little and worked 20 meters
from there.

I made 12 CW QSOs from 16:55 to 17:38 UTC. The phone started getting hot in the
middle of the activation, but the stop still stayed reasonably quick: a little
over half an hour on the air, maybe 45 minutes total with setup and teardown.
Given the heat, the refuge rules, and the need to keep moving south, the picnic
table and hitch-supported wire were perfect.

## [Jekyll Island State Park, US-0636](/radio/pota/US-0636/)

Once Savannah was done, we stopped to charge and then drove to Jekyll Island.
Nearly the whole island is the state park, and our hotel was inside the park
boundary, which made the Georgia finish unusually convenient.

We got there in the late afternoon. I still had a few hours of work and a
5 p.m. meeting, so I set up at the room table for a while after check-in. Around
7 p.m., Max and I headed down to the beach for a quick activation.

For this one I used the KX2 at 5 W with the 25-foot whip in a Rybakov-style
setup. The beach was doing what I hoped it would do. Salt water in front of the
antenna plus gray-line timing made 20 meters feel alive, and strong hunters
kept coming.

I made 20 CW QSOs on 20 meters from 23:15 to 23:48 UTC. That was right around
30 minutes of operating, and it felt like the easiest run of the day. A lot of
people came by to ask what I was doing, including one gentleman who stayed for
15 or 20 minutes. He was an electrical engineer, so the conversation about
propagation and antennas had a different but very useful angle.

After that we called it for the night, walked back to the hotel, and got food.
The goal for the next morning was to get back out to the beach early before the
drive farther into Florida.

## What worked

- Adding Woods Bay removed the ambiguity around getting South Carolina into the
  trip record before Savannah NWR.
- Morning 40 meters did exactly what I wanted at Woods Bay: close-in coverage
  and enough hunters for a quick activation.
- Abercorn Landing was a good Savannah NWR operating spot. Local advice saved a
  lot of searching.
- The hitch-supported mast and random wire stayed useful for fast travel-day
  stops, while the KX2 made the picnic-table activation simple.
- The Jekyll Island beach setup was the best radio moment of the day: 5 W, a
  25-foot whip, salt water, gray line, and steady 20-meter hunters.

## To adjust next time

- Keep the phone shaded or cooled during hot activations. It was the weak point
  at Savannah, not the radio.
- Write down the CW paddle and operating ergonomics for every stop, even when
  the activation is quick.
- For refuge activations, keep using self-contained supports and simple
  operating positions unless local rules clearly allow more.
